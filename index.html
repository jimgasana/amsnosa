Program Assessment Narrative
Rwanda Nuclear Program Status / April 2026 SOURCED

Rwanda has completed the IAEA Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure Review Phase 1 mission, conducted 2 to 9 March 2026. The ten-member team assessed Rwanda's readiness across all 19 infrastructure areas required for safe, secure and sustainable nuclear development. The mission confirmed Rwanda is making significant progress and identified strong government commitment and early emergency preparedness as good practices that could benefit other countries.

The IAEA recommended three priority actions: finalize the comprehensive national decision-making report, complete the review of national legislation, and continue developing strategic policies essential for the long-term program. The comprehensive nuclear energy law covering licensing, safety, waste and liability was drafted in 2025 and is currently under legislative review.

Rwanda's current installed electricity capacity stands at approximately 650 to 700 MW. The country targets nuclear power supplying 60 to 70 percent of its electricity mix in the medium and long term. Rwanda's deployment strategy centres on SMR units of 110 to 150 MW per module, which aligns with projected demand growth to 2.5 to 4.5 GW by mid-century. Technology partnerships include Dual Fluid Energy (September 2023) and NANO Nuclear Energy (August 2024). The program holds significance beyond Rwanda's borders as a proof-of-concept model for African nuclear adoption.

Sources: IAEA INIR Press Release March 2026 iaea.org / Energy Capital Power March 2026 energycapitalpower.com / Nuclear Business Platform March 2026 nuclearbusiness-platform.com

Plain Language: Nuclear Energy Questions Answered
Why is Rwanda pursuing nuclear energy?
Rwanda currently generates approximately 650 to 700 MW of electricity. Demand is projected to grow to 2.5 to 4.5 GW by mid-century. Hydropower generates about 43.9 percent of current supply but is vulnerable to climate-driven rainfall variability. Nuclear provides stable, baseload electricity that does not depend on weather conditions. The government targets nuclear supplying 60 to 70 percent of the national electricity mix in the long term.
What type of nuclear reactor will Rwanda build?
Rwanda's strategy focuses on Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) with individual units generating between 110 and 150 MW of electricity. These are smaller, scalable reactors that can be built incrementally as demand grows. Rwanda has signed technology cooperation agreements with Dual Fluid Energy of Canada and Germany, and NANO Nuclear Energy of the United States. No final technology vendor has been selected yet. The IAEA is supporting Rwanda's vendor prequalification process.
Is nuclear power safe?
Modern SMR designs selected by Rwanda feature passive safety systems that stop and cool the reactor automatically without requiring electricity or human action. Nuclear power has one of the lowest rates of deaths per unit of energy produced of any energy source. Rwanda's program will comply with all IAEA safety standards. The IAEA has already identified Rwanda's early emergency preparedness planning as a good practice. A national training exercise for first responders to radiological emergencies has been conducted.
What will happen to the nuclear waste?
The volume of waste from a nuclear plant is very small compared to other energy sources. All high-level spent fuel is solid, sealed, and managed in licensed storage facilities. Rwanda's comprehensive nuclear law, drafted in 2025, includes specific provisions for radioactive waste management. Long-term waste disposal will be part of the fuel supply agreement with the technology vendor.
How can Rwandan citizens participate in this process?
The IAEA Milestones Approach requires formal public consultation during the site selection and licensing process. Rwanda's Minister of Infrastructure has confirmed the government's commitment to transparent development. The Rwanda Atomic Energy Board (RAEB) is the public authority responsible for the programme. Citizens can contact RAEB at KG 13 Avenue 20, Nyarutarama, Kigali, or through the official website raeb.gov.rw.
