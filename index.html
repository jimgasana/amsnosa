Walk through Rwanda's nuclear journey chapter by chapter. From the founding decision in 2020 to the first reactor online in the early 2030s. Designed for presentations, briefings, and first-time visitors.
2020
CHAPTER 1
THE DECISION TO GO NUCLEAR
In December 2020, Rwanda made a decisive commitment. Presidential Order 127/01 formally established the Rwanda Atomic Energy Board, the institutional engine of a national nuclear programme. This was not a study or an expression of interest. It was a structural act that embedded nuclear energy into Rwanda's sovereign development architecture. Nuclear power was written into the national energy policy, the national development strategy, and the National Land-Use Master Plan. President Kagame's framing was direct: nuclear is the platform for industrial growth, not just a power source.

CHAPTER 2
BUILDING THE INSTITUTIONS
RAEB was not created as a signalling device. It was built as an operating institution. Rwanda partnered with universities to launch nuclear engineering programmes. First responder training exercises for radiological emergencies were conducted nationally. The University of Rwanda College of Science and Technology launched dedicated nuclear tracks in partnership with RAEB in December 2023. A DELIBERATE GLOBAL PARTNERSHIP STRATEGY

CHAPTER 4
THE IAEA ENDORSEMENT
From 2 to 9 March 2026, a ten-member IAEA team conducted the Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure Review in Kigali, assessing all 19 infrastructure areas required for safe nuclear development. EDUCATION / INTERACTIVE EXPLAINERS
LEARNING HUB
For students, educators, policymakers, and any first-time visitor. Plain-language explainers on nuclear energy, the IAEA process, financing structures, and why Rwanda's approach is distinctive.
01
Fission: The Core Process
Nuclear energy is produced by splitting uranium atoms in a controlled process called fission. When a uranium-235 atom absorbs a neutron, it splits into smaller atoms and releases a large amount of energy as heat. No combustion occurs, so there are no carbon dioxide emissions during operation.
02
Heat to Electricity
The heat from fission is used to produce steam. Steam drives a turbine connected to a generator, which produces electricity. This is the same process used in coal and gas plants, but the fuel source is nuclear rather than fossil fuels.
03
The Chain Reaction
Each fission event releases additional neutrons, which can split more uranium atoms. This self-sustaining chain reaction is carefully controlled by inserting or withdrawing control rods. A nuclear plant cannot explode like a nuclear bomb because the fuel is not enriched to the required level.
04
Waste Volume
The volume of high-level nuclear waste produced is remarkably small. All the spent fuel ever produced by US commercial nuclear reactors would fit inside a single Walmart. The waste is solid, dense, and can be safely stored in sealed containers for very long periods.
01
What is an SMR
A Small Modular Reactor generates between 10 and 300 MW of electricity, compared to 1,000 MW or more for a conventional large reactor. SMRs are built in factories and transported to site, reducing construction time and cost. Rwanda's target is units of 110 to 150 MW per module.
02
Why SMRs Suit Africa
Africa's electricity grids are smaller than those in Europe or North America. A 1,000 MW reactor would overwhelm most African grids. SMRs can be scaled incrementally, adding capacity as demand grows. They require less water and have smaller site footprints, making them suitable for landlocked countries like Rwanda.
03
Passive Safety
Most SMR designs feature passive safety systems. In an emergency, cooling happens automatically through physics: gravity, natural circulation, and heat transfer, without needing electricity or human action. The reactor shuts down safely on its own, unlike older reactor designs.
04
Financing Advantage
A 77 MW SMR module might cost USD 200 to 300 million, compared to USD 10 billion or more for a large conventional reactor. This lower upfront cost makes financing more accessible. Countries can also stage investment, adding modules as revenue from early units begins to flow.
01
The Milestones Approach
The IAEA provides a structured three-phase framework for countries developing nuclear programmes. Phase 1 is Consider: readiness to commit. Phase 2 is Prepare: ready to invite bids. Phase 3 is Construct: ready to commission. Rwanda has completed Phase 1.
02
The INIR Mission
An Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure Review is a formal assessment conducted by international experts appointed by the IAEA. They evaluate the country across 19 specific infrastructure areas including safety, security, safeguards, legal framework, waste management, and public information. Rwanda's INIR took place 2 to 9 March 2026.
03
The 19 Infrastructure Areas
These include: national position, nuclear safety, management, funding and financing, legal framework, safeguards, regulatory framework, radiation protection, electrical grid, human resource development, stakeholder involvement, site and supporting facilities, environmental protection, emergency planning, nuclear security, nuclear fuel cycle, radioactive waste management, industrial involvement, and procurement.
04
What Good Practices Mean
When the IAEA identifies a good practice, it means Rwanda's approach in that area is considered a model that other countries should study and replicate. The March 2026 mission identified Rwanda's government coordination, stakeholder engagement, and emergency preparedness as good practices. This is a significant institutional endorsement.
01
The Financing Challenge
Nuclear plants have high upfront capital costs, typically 70 to 85 percent fixed before a single kilowatt-hour is generated. This front-loaded cost profile is the primary barrier for developing countries. SMRs reduce this barrier significantly, with a 100 MW unit estimated at USD 200 to 300 million vs USD 5 to 10 billion for a large reactor.
02
Blended Finance
Most nuclear projects in emerging markets use blended finance: a combination of government equity, development finance institution (DFI) loans, export credit agency (ECA) guarantees from the vendor country, and commercial debt. Each layer de-risks the investment for the next. Rwanda's model will likely follow this structure.
03
Power Purchase Agreements
A PPA is a long-term contract between the plant owner and an electricity buyer, typically 20 to 30 years. PPAs provide revenue certainty, which enables lenders to commit financing. Industrial buyers such as mining companies and data centres are natural anchor off-takers because they need 24-hour reliable power.
04
Build-Own-Operate Models
Some vendor countries, particularly Russia and China, offer Build-Own-Operate financing where the vendor finances, builds, owns, and operates the plant, with the host country paying per kilowatt-hour over decades. This reduces upfront risk for the host country but creates long-term strategic dependencies.
01
The Energy Deficit
600 million Africans have no access to electricity. Africa's total electricity generation is expected to grow sevenfold by 2050. The continent produces 14 percent of global uranium supply from Namibia, Niger, and South Africa. The resources, the need, and the technology are converging.
02
The SMR Window
SMR technology is reaching commercial readiness at exactly the moment when African energy demand is accelerating. Countries that move now on regulatory preparation, workforce development, and site selection will be positioned to deploy in the early 2030s. Countries that wait face a 10 to 15 year delay from standing start.
03
Industrial Demand as Anchor
Africa's mining sector, growing data centre industry, and manufacturing zones need 24-hour baseload power that solar and wind cannot reliably provide. These industrial consumers are natural anchor buyers for nuclear power. Their PPAs provide the revenue certainty that unlocks financing.
04
The Rwanda Proof-of-Concept
A successful Rwandan SMR deployment would be the first reference plant in a small African grid context. This reduces technology risk perception for every subsequent African program. The commercial and geopolitical value of being first is enormous. Rwanda is not just building a power plant; it is building the template.
OK
Deaths per unit of energy (lowest of all sources)Nuclear energy produces fewer deaths per terawatt-hour than any other energy source, including rooftop solar and wind. Coal produces approximately 2,500 times more deaths per unit of energy than nuclear.
OK
Site boundary radiation dose (typical SMR)The radiation dose at the boundary of a licensed nuclear plant is approximately 0.1 mSv per year. The global average natural background radiation is 2.4 mSv per year. A flight from Kigali to London exposes a passenger to approximately 0.05 to 0.1 mSv.
OK
Passive safety eliminates station blackout riskModern SMR designs shut down and cool automatically without power, pumps, or human action. This directly addresses the primary failure mode of older reactor designs. The IAEA's safety standards require this passive capability for all new reactor designs seeking international approval.
OK
Physical security and safeguardsAll nuclear facilities operate under IAEA safeguards, meaning regular international inspections verify that nuclear materials are used only for peaceful purposes. Rwanda's nuclear law includes specific safeguards provisions aligned with IAEA requirements.
OK
Rwanda's first-responder trainingRwanda has already conducted a five-day national training and field simulation exercise for first responders to radiological emergencies. The IAEA cited this as a good practice in the March 2026 mission.

Plain Language Questions and Answers
Why is Rwanda pursuing nuclear energy?Rwanda currently generates approximately 650 to 700 MW. Demand will grow to 2.5 to 4.5 GW by mid-century. Hydropower (43.9% of supply) is vulnerable to rainfall variability. Nuclear provides stable, 24-hour baseload electricity with no carbon emissions and predictable long-term pricing.
What type of reactor will Rwanda build?Small Modular Reactors of 110 to 150 MW per unit. Rwanda has signed technology cooperation agreements with Dual Fluid Energy and NANO Nuclear Energy. No vendor has been selected yet. RAEB is conducting formal prequalification of Generation III+ and advanced SMR designs.
Is nuclear power safe for Rwanda?Modern SMR designs selected by Rwanda feature passive safety systems that shut down and cool automatically without power or human action. The IAEA cited Rwanda's early emergency preparedness as a good practice. A national first-responder radiological training exercise has been completed.
How can citizens participate?The IAEA Milestones Approach requires formal public consultation during site selection and licensing. The Rwanda Atomic Energy Board (RAEB) is the responsible public authority. Contact: KG 13 Avenue 20, Nyarutarama, Kigali. Website: raeb.gov.rw.

ANALYSIS
Rwanda is demonstrating that grid size and infrastructure starting point are not the binding constraints on nuclear adoption. A successful Rwanda SMR deployment provides the technology sector with an operating reference plant in an emerging market context, the most commercially valuable asset any new nuclear technology can possess.
